Most app ideas do not arrive fully dressed. They turn up as sentence fragments, dumb jokes, or strange little "what ifs" your brain produces while making toast.

"What if you could hide encrypted messages inside LEGO brick images?"
"What if a song could generate printable artwork?"
"What if an app could make a full MIDI song from a genre prompt?"

This is where ideas usually die. Not because they are bad, but because they are foggy. They are vibes wearing a fake moustache and pretending to be a product.

AI coding tools make this more interesting, and also more dangerous. They can build quickly, but they are not mind readers. If you give them a vague idea, they will build a vague app. Sometimes a surprisingly confident vague app, with buttons, gradients, and the structural integrity of wet cereal.

The useful skill is not having ideas. Ideas are cheap. I have seventeen before breakfast and most of them should be contained. The useful skill is turning the idea into something buildable.

## Start with the stupid version

I try not to make the first version sound impressive. That is harder than it should be, because the instinct is to dress the idea up like it is pitching for seed funding in a room full of expensive shoes.

Do not do that.

"An app that turns messages into LEGO brick pictures, and later lets you decode them" is better than "a playful cryptographic visual communication platform leveraging modular brick-based steganographic aesthetics." The second one sounds like it was generated by a dishwasher with a LinkedIn account. The first one tells you what the thing does.

At this stage, I want one or two plain sentences. Enough to give the idea a spine.

## Find the actual interaction

A lot of app ideas are secretly just outputs. "Generate artwork." "Make a MIDI file." "Encode a message." That is not an app yet. That is a vending machine.

The app is the interaction around the output. What does the user do first? What do they see while it is working? What do they get at the end? What happens if it fails? What makes them want to try again?

Take the LEGO cryptography idea. The output is a PNG image. Fine. But the interaction is the product: write a secret message, enter a password, encrypt it, generate a pleasing abstract LEGO object, download the image, then later upload it and decode the message.

Now it has a shape. Not a perfect one, but it has hands and feet. It can walk around a bit.

## Set the rules of the game

This might be the most important part. AI tools are very good at adding things. They will happily give your tiny weird app user accounts, dashboards, analytics, onboarding tours, Stripe integration, and a testimonial carousel from imaginary people named "Sarah K."

You need constraints. No login unless absolutely necessary. No database unless the app truly needs persistence. No AI image generation if the concept should be algorithmic. Must run on Vercel serverless. Must work on mobile. Must produce downloadable files. Must be understandable from the first screen.

This is a bit like building a Magic: The Gathering deck. The interesting part is not having access to every card ever printed. That way lies nonsense, bankruptcy, and a shoebox full of goblins. The interesting part is choosing a format, a colour identity, a win condition, and the cards you are deliberately not playing.

Apps are the same. Constraints are not boring. They are what stop the thing becoming a cursed train.

## Turn vibes into requirements

Vibes are allowed. I like vibes. Aesthetic direction matters, especially for small creative tools. But vibes need translation.

"Make it whimsical" is not enough. Whimsical how? Soft colours? Bouncy motion? Hand-drawn icons? Casual copy? Rounded cards? Does it look like a children's book, a toy catalogue, a 90s CD-ROM, or a slightly unwell spreadsheet?

Instead of "late 90s hacking vibe," write: "Dark panels, chunky pixel-inspired borders, faux terminal elements, sparse neon accents, monospace labels, playful warning copy, and simple animated scan states. Avoid modern SaaS minimalism."

That gives the AI something to build. It also tells it what to avoid, which is often more useful. Left unattended, AI will make everything look like a password manager for a Scandinavian bank.

## Write the PRD like instructions for a very fast intern

An AI-readable PRD does not need to be fancy. Fancy is usually bad. The goal is not to impress a steering committee. The goal is to reduce ambiguity.

I write PRDs as if I am handing the project to a capable intern who works very fast, has read the entire internet, and occasionally forgets what gravity is.

The structure is simple: product name, one-line summary, goal, target user, core journey, functional requirements, technical constraints, UI direction, edge cases, and acceptance criteria.

The acceptance criteria matter. "The app should generate nice artwork" is useless. "The app should generate a deterministic PNG from the uploaded audio, and the same audio file should always produce the same image" is testable. Now the AI has a target, and so do you.

## Name the hard parts

A lot of AI-built app projects go wrong because the PRD says something breezy like, "The app generates a finished, professional-quality song."

Right. Of course. Just pop that in a button.

Some things are hard. Strong encryption is hard. Good procedural music is hard. Beautiful deterministic art is hard. Running everything inside serverless limits can be hard. Accessibility is not a garnish.

A good PRD names the traps. "Use standard browser cryptography APIs. Do not invent a custom encryption algorithm." Or: "Image generation must be algorithmic and deterministic. Do not use AI image generation."

This is not pessimism. It is putting little flags next to the holes.

## Build the smallest impressive version

I do not mean an MVP where you remove everything joyful and ship a beige apology. I mean the smallest version that proves the idea.

For the LEGO cryptography app, that might be: write message, encrypt it, generate PNG, upload PNG, decrypt message. No accounts. No gallery. No teams. No social graph. Please, for the love of small apps, no social graph.

The mistake is trying to build the fantasy version first. That way lies a half-finished settings panel and a deep sense of personal failure.

Small and complete beats ambitious and haunted.

## The PRD is the spellbook

The weird idea is the spark. The PRD is how you stop the spark from setting fire to the curtains.

The process is simple: capture the plain idea, define the core interaction, set constraints, translate vibes into buildable requirements, name the hard parts, define acceptance criteria, build the smallest impressive version, then test it like a suspicious raccoon.

That is how a vague concept becomes a buildable app. Not by making it bigger. Usually by making it smaller, clearer, and less able to escape through the vents.

The best weird little apps do not need to become startups. They do not need pricing pages, growth loops, or inspirational launch posts with laptops near plants. Some apps can just be useful, playful, or strange little artefacts that exist because the idea made you grin for three seconds and you had enough discipline to turn it into a thing.

That is enough. Honestly, it might be better.
