I have a lot of hobbies. LEGO, music production, photography, painting, writing. None of them are related to each other in any obvious way, and none of them are coding. For a long time, I treated them as separate lives: code during the day, hobbies at night, never the twain shall meet.

That was a mistake.

## The Problem with Problem Statements

Most developers I know struggle with the same thing: "what should I build?" Not technically, we can figure out the how. The hard part is the *what*. What's worth spending evenings and weekends on? What problem is actually interesting enough to sustain months of work?

The standard advice is "scratch your own itch." Find something that annoys you and fix it. And that's fine advice, but it has a ceiling. Most of the things that annoy me about daily life have already been solved by a dozen apps. I don't need another to-do list. I don't need another habit tracker.

What I needed was a better source of problem statements. And it was sitting in front of me the whole time, scattered across my apartment in the form of half-assembled LEGO sets, MIDI controllers, and oil paintings drying on the shelf.

## The Overlap Zone

Here's the thing about hobbies: they're full of friction. Not the bad kind, the *interesting* kind. The kind that makes you think "I wish there was a better way to do this" or "what if I could combine these two things" or "why does this have to be so manual?"

LEGO is about modular construction and spatial reasoning. Music production is about signal processing, pattern generation, and creative constraints. Photography is about composition, light, and perspective. Painting is about color theory, texture, and happy accidents. Writing is about structure, voice, and editing.

Every single one of those words (modular, signal, pattern, composition, texture, structure) is also a software concept. The overlap is already there. You just have to look for it.

## How Splinched Happened

Splinched started because I was frustrated with sample chopping.

I produce music as a hobby. When you're working with samples, taking a piece of audio and slicing it into smaller pieces to rearrange, the process is tedious. You load a file into a DAW, manually find the slice points, manually rearrange them, manually audition each variation. It's slow, it's repetitive, and it requires a lot of musical knowledge to do well.

I kept thinking: what if the computer could do the boring parts? What if I could drop in a sound and get back dozens of slice variations automatically? What if the rearrangement patterns were generated algorithmically instead of manually?

That question, born entirely from my hobby, from hours of manual sample chopping that I found tedious, became a full application. Browser-based audio analysis, multiple chopping algorithms, 128+ sequencer patterns, MIDI integration, WAV export. A real tool that solves a real problem.

The key insight was that I didn't start by thinking "I want to build a web app." I started by thinking "I want sample chopping to be less annoying." The technology followed the problem, not the other way around.

## Why Hobbies Make Better Problems

When you pull problem statements from your hobbies instead of from "what would look good on a resume," three things change:

**1. You actually understand the domain.**

I've spent years chopping samples by hand. I know what's annoying about it, what workflows feel natural, what features matter and what doesn't. That domain knowledge is impossible to fake and invaluable when designing a tool. Most side projects fail not because the code is bad, but because the developer doesn't deeply understand the problem they're solving.

**2. You have built-in motivation.**

Building Splinched wasn't a chore. I was solving *my own* problem. Every feature I added was something I wanted to use. That intrinsic motivation carried me through the hard parts: the audio processing edge cases, the cross-browser compatibility issues, the tedious UI work. If I'd been building yet another CRUD app for a hypothetical user, I would have quit halfway through.

**3. You can test it immediately.**

I am the user. I can load my own samples, try my own workflows, and know within seconds whether something works or feels wrong. No user interviews, no persona documents, no guessing. Just me, my music, and the tool.

## The Pattern

Once I recognized this pattern in Splinched, I started seeing it everywhere:

- **Photography** → tools for composition analysis, color palette extraction from photos, automated culling of burst shots
- **LEGO** → inventory management for brick collections, set recommendation engines, MOC (My Own Creation) instruction generators
- **Painting** → color mixing simulators, palette generators from reference images, texture overlay tools
- **Writing** → distraction-free editors with unusual constraints, prose rhythm analyzers, outline visualization tools

None of these are "solved" problems in the way that to-do lists or note-taking apps are. They're niche, personal, and rooted in real frustration from real practice. That's what makes them worth building.

## The Approach

If you want to try this yourself, here's the framework I've been using:

1. **Pick a hobby you spend real time on.** Not one you're vaguely interested in, but one where you have actual hours invested and actual frustrations accumulated.
2. **Notice the friction.** What's tedious? What's repetitive? What requires knowledge you wish you didn't need? What's manual that feels like it shouldn't be?
3. **Look for the computational angle.** Where does pattern matching, signal processing, procedural generation, data visualization, or automation apply?
4. **Build the minimum tool.** Not a platform. Not a SaaS. Just a tool that solves one specific friction point for yourself.
5. **Use it.** Actually use it. If it's good, you'll keep using it. If it's not, you'll learn why.

The goal isn't to build a startup. It's to build something you actually want to exist, and to learn a lot in the process.

## The Point

Your hobbies aren't distractions from your coding career. They're a treasure trove of domain expertise, motivation, and problem statements that most developers ignore because they don't look "professional" or "enterprise-ready."

The best projects I've built came from the intersection of "I care about this" and "I can build this." That intersection is wider than you think. You just have to stop treating your hobbies and your code as separate lives.

They're not. They're the same life. Let them overlap.
