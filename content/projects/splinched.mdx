## Overview

Splinched is a web-based sample chopping and sequencing tool built for music producers. You upload or record a piece of audio, and the app automatically slices it using a variety of algorithms — transient detection, beat-synced, random, even-spaced, and more. Each algorithm produces a different set of slices, and every variant can be previewed independently.

The slices are then loaded into a step sequencer with 128+ built-in patterns — half rhythmic, half melodic — each with 16, 32, or 64 steps. The output can be previewed in the browser and downloaded as 16-bit WAV files, individually or bundled in a ZIP.

The core idea is that chopping samples shouldn't require music theory knowledge or a pre-existing vision. Splinched embraces randomness and happy accidents. You feed it audio, it gives you back something you wouldn't have thought to make.

## The Problem It Solves

Traditional sample chopping in DAWs like Ableton or on hardware like the SP-404 requires the producer to already know what they want the output to sound like. You need to understand transients, bar structure, and rhythmic phrasing to make meaningful chops. Tools like XLN Audio's Life plugin enable a more random workflow, but they're designed for drum loops and have limited sequencer pattern diversity.

Splinched removes that barrier. It applies every supported chopping algorithm to your audio simultaneously, so you can audition dozens of slice variations without manual work. The sequencer patterns range from straightforward drum patterns to complex melodic sequences that re-pitch slices according to a detected or user-specified key and mode.

## Development Process

The project started with the audio engine. The Web Audio API's `AudioContext` and `OfflineAudioContext` handle all playback and rendering, but the real complexity lives in the slicing algorithms.

### Chopping Algorithms

The app supports at least 6 chopping methods, each taking a different approach to dividing audio:

- **Transients** — Detects amplitude peaks in the waveform to find natural attack points. This is the default algorithm and works well for most musical material.
- **Beat-Synced** — Divides the audio based on the detected or user-set BPM, slicing at musically relevant grid positions.
- **Random** — Places slice boundaries at random positions, weighted toward areas of higher amplitude.
- **Even** — Divides the audio into equal-length segments regardless of content.
- **Frequency-Based** — Uses spectral analysis to find points where the frequency content changes significantly.
- **Onset Detection** — A hybrid approach that combines amplitude and spectral flux to detect note onsets.

Each algorithm runs independently, and the user can switch between them to compare outputs. The slice count is configurable: 2, 4, 6, 8, 16, 32, 64, or 256 slices, with 16 as the default.

### Sequencer Patterns

The sequencer is the heart of the app. It has 128+ patterns split evenly between rhythmic and melodic categories:

- **Rhythmic patterns** are weighted toward slices identified as drums. They include standard drum patterns, syncopated grooves, and polyrhythmic sequences. Some include micro-timing offsets (random ±0–30ms shifts) and ratchets (rapid repeated notes) for humanization.
- **Melodic patterns** limit the pitch of slices according to a user-selectable key, mode (major, minor, dorian, etc.), and octave. Slices identified as drums are automatically excluded from melodic patterns. Melodic patterns play slices chromatically within the constraints of the selected scale.

Patterns can be 16, 32, or 64 steps. Steps wrap to new lines in the display. A live playhead tracks playback across the waveform preview and the sequencer grid simultaneously.

### Pattern Manipulation

Beyond selecting a pattern, users have deep control over how it plays:

- **Density slider** — Adjusts the ratio of active to inactive steps. Acts as a non-destructive mask that preserves manual edits underneath.
- **Syncopation slider** — Controls the number of repeated slices in the pattern. Higher values = more repetition.
- **Swing control** — Shifts timing of off-beat steps for groove adjustment.
- **Euclidean generator** — Generates Euclidean rhythms on the fly with visible Steps and Pulses controls.
- **Re-roll button** — Randomizes which slices are assigned to which active steps in the sequencer.
- **Note Repeat** — Percentage likelihood of a step having a ratcheted/repeated note (default 0%).
- **Micro-Timing** — Percentage likelihood of a step being offset by a random 0–30ms amount (default 0%).
- **Probability** — Percentage likelihood that each step is triggered at all (default 100%).

### ADSR Envelope

A user-controlled ADSR (Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release) envelope shapes every sample played through the sequencer. The sliders are logarithmic, with a range of 0–1000ms. Default values: A 20ms, D 150ms, S 0ms, R 80ms. The envelope is visualized as a curve where each section's length reflects its current value, and the total duration is displayed inside the visualization.

An "Allow Deviation" toggle introduces per-slice randomness: when active, each slice's envelope deviates by ±50% from the set values.

### Audio Analysis

Before the sequencer can make intelligent decisions, each slice needs to be analyzed:

- **Pitch detection** — Each slice's pitch is analyzed and displayed on the sequencer steps and waveform preview.
- **Instrument classification** — Slices are categorized by instrument type (drums, tonal, etc.), which determines how they're weighted in rhythmic vs. melodic patterns.
- **Key and mode detection** — The uploaded audio's musical key and mode are auto-detected and displayed (e.g., "Auto-detected: D Minor"). The user can override this.

### Filtering

Three-band EQ controls (Low, Mid, High) as vertical sliders, defaulting to 50%. Values above 50% boost, below 50% cut. This shapes the tonal character of the output before export.

## Technology

The entire app runs client-side in the browser. No server, no backend, no external dependencies at runtime.

### Web Audio API

All audio processing — playback, slicing, envelope shaping, filtering, and rendering for export — uses the Web Audio API. `AudioContext` handles real-time playback, while `OfflineAudioContext` renders exports at the correct sample rate without blocking the UI.

### Web MIDI API

MIDI connectivity is initialized on demand via a button press. Once connected, a MIDI keyboard can trigger individual slices chromatically. The app maps MIDI note numbers to slice indices with pitch transposition.

### Canvas API

The waveform display is rendered on a `<canvas>` element. Slice boundaries are drawn as vertical lines over the waveform. When a slice is triggered by the sequencer, it gets a visual highlight that persists for the duration of the slice's playback. Clicking a slice in the waveform toggles its corresponding steps in the sequencer.

Slices that are used in active sequencer steps are highlighted in the waveform preview at all times, giving a visual map of which parts of the audio are actually being used.

### Drag and Drop

The app supports a full drag-and-drop workflow:
- Drag a slice from the waveform onto a sequencer step
- Drag a slice from the waveform onto a pad in the slice bank
- Drag a slice from the slice bank onto a sequencer step
- Drag a slice from a sequencer step back onto a pad

### Persistence

All settings, uploaded audio, and generated patterns persist in `localStorage`. Refreshing the page restores the full state — the audio, the current pattern, all slider positions, and any manually edited steps. Users can save named sequences to localStorage and recall them from a dropdown.

### Export

Output is rendered as 16-bit WAV files using a custom WAV encoder. Users can download individual samples or bundle everything into a single ZIP file using JSZip. The export includes a configurable loop count: the sequence rendered 1, 2, 4, 8, or 16 times.

### Keyboard Shortcuts

Every control has a visible keyboard shortcut. All shortcuts are displayed on-screen. The app is fully keyboard-navigable and A11y compliant.

## UI Design

The interface is designed to look and feel like professional music software — dense with controls, but organized. All controls are accessible within a single click, not buried in menus or context actions.

Each UI section (waveform, sequencer, ADSR, filters, patterns) can be collapsed and expanded by clicking its heading. Collapsed sections are visually distinct so it's clear they can be expanded.

Sliders show visual feedback: when at a default value, they're greyed out. When deviating from default, a line shows the direction and magnitude of the deviation. All sliders have a small snap zone around their default value.

The sequencer steps wrap to new lines as needed. Each step shows its slice number and, for melodic patterns, its musical note. Steps can be toggled by clicking or by clicking and dragging across multiple steps. Micro-timing and ratchets are visually indicated on the steps where they apply.

The UI is optimized for mobile — all controls remain accessible on a smartphone screen.

## What I Learned

- The Web Audio API is incredibly capable but has sharp edges around timing. `AudioContext.currentTime` is your source of truth, and scheduling ahead with `noteOn`/`noteOff` is essential for tight playback.
- Pitch detection in the browser is hard. Simple autocorrelation works for monophonic material but breaks down on complex mixes. For polyphonic audio, you need spectral analysis.
- `localStorage` has a ~5MB limit. Storing raw audio data requires encoding it efficiently (base64-encoded WAV) and being mindful of quota.
- Building a sequencer UI that works equally well with mouse, touch, and MIDI requires careful event handling. Pointer events abstract over all three input methods.

## Challenges

- **Latency**: Getting slice playback to feel immediate required pre-loading all slice buffers into memory and scheduling playback slightly ahead of the audio clock.
- **ADSR visualization**: Rendering the logarithmic envelope curve in real-time as sliders move required a custom canvas renderer that maps logarithmic values to screen coordinates.
- **Pattern storage**: 128+ patterns with variable step counts, note assignments, micro-timing, and ratchets is a lot of data. Compressing the pattern format while keeping it human-readable was a design challenge.
- **Drag and drop across contexts**: Dragging a slice from the waveform (canvas) to a sequencer step (DOM) requires careful coordinate mapping and custom drag state management.
